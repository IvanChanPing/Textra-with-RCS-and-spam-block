================================================================
  WIRING THE MESSAGING ENGINE INTO YOUR APP
  (plain-language guide — for a human developer)
================================================================

WHAT THIS GUIDE IS
------------------
Guide 1 got the Rust engine into your project — the `.so`, the Kotlin
bindings, the `RustClient` class. That is the TRANSPORT: it can pair,
send, and receive over Google Messages' "Messages for web" protocol.

But a transport on its own does not make a messaging app. Something has
to DRIVE it (decide when to send), CONSUME it (react to what comes back),
and WRITE the results somewhere the user can see them. That glue is the
subject of this guide.

TextRCS is a modified build of the Textra SMS app. It cannot touch
Textra's source — Textra ships only as a compiled APK — so every place
TextRCS connects the engine to the app is done by editing decompiled
"smali" bytecode. That is a workaround forced by not having the source.

YOU have your source. So you do NOT smali-patch anything. Wherever this
guide says "TextRCS patches X in smali", the instruction for you is the
plain-Kotlin equivalent: call a method from your own code. This guide
walks each of those, names the real TextRCS class that does the work,
and tells you what its source-code counterpart is.

Read this AFTER Guide 1. The engine must already build and the
`uniffi.textrcs_libgm.RustClient` class must be importable.


THE ARCHITECTURE — FOUR LAYERS
------------------------------
Picture the finished system as four layers, top to bottom:

  1. YOUR APP'S UI + MESSAGE STORE
        Your conversation list, your chat screen, your database.
        This is yours; the engine never touches it directly.

  2. THE GLUE LAYER  ← this guide builds this
        A handful of small classes that translate between your app
        and the engine: a send driver, a receive handler, a thin
        "write it into my store" adapter, a pairing screen.

  3. THE RUST CLIENT  (`uniffi.textrcs_libgm.RustClient`)
        The transport. You constructed this in Guide 1. It owns the
        long-poll, the keep-alive pinger, encryption, and the
        send RPCs. You call into it; it calls back out to you.

  4. GOOGLE'S SERVERS / the user's paired phone.

The glue layer (layer 2) is what TextRCS keeps in
`inject_src/com/textrcs/` — included in this kit under
`App Integration Source/com/textrcs/`. The most important classes, and
what each is FOR, are:

  bridge/RustBridge.kt
        The single point where the app meets the engine. Holds the
        one `RustClient`, starts/stops it, exposes `sendText(...)`,
        and — crucially — implements `RustEventSink`, the callback
        the engine fires for every incoming frame.

  send/SendManager.kt
        The send driver. Your "send" button ends up here. It hands
        off to `RustBridge.sendText`.

  receive/ReceiveService.kt
        An Android foreground Service whose only job is to keep the
        process alive (and the long-poll open) while the app is in
        the background, and to start/stop `RustBridge`.

  receive/IncomingMessageHandler.kt
        The receive brain. Parses each incoming engine frame, works
        out who sent it, whether it is a group, whether it is one of
        the user's own messages from another device, and routes it.

  bridge/TextraDbBridge.kt
        The "write it into the store" adapter. THIS ONE IS
        TEXTRA-SPECIFIC — it reflects into Textra's private SQLite
        database and UI. In your app you replace it wholesale with
        the equivalent against YOUR store. Treat it as a reference
        for WHAT to write, not as a drop-in.

  ui/pairing/PairingActivity.kt
        The Google sign-in screen (a WebView) plus the handshake
        that produces a paired session.

  ui/TypingIndicator.kt
        Shows a "typing…" affordance when the other party is typing.

  control/Hooks.kt
        A debug switch registry. In the shipping build it is inert
        (every `shouldSkip` returns false). You can ignore it — the
        `Hooks.shouldSkip(...)` calls peppered through the code are
        no-ops. Do not let them distract you.

  gmproto/
        Generated Java protobuf classes for the Google Messages wire
        format (conversations, events, rpc, …). Generated code — you
        do not edit it; you just parse engine bytes with it (e.g.
        `UpdateEvents.parseFrom(...)`).


HOW SEND AND RECEIVE ACTUALLY FLOW
----------------------------------
SEND (user taps "send" in your app):

   your send code
     → SendManager.sendText(phone, body, sentIntents?)
       → (off a background thread)
       → RustBridge.sendText(context, phone, body)
         → RustClient.sendText(phone, body)   [the engine]
   Then SendManager fires your "sent / failed" callback.

RECEIVE (a message arrives, or the engine has news):

   RustClient long-poll  [the engine]
     → RustBridge.Sink.onDataEvent(action, decrypted, ...)   [your RustEventSink]
       → UpdateEvents.parseFrom(decrypted)
       → IncomingMessageHandler.onUpdateEvents(context, events)
         → resolves sender / group / own-send
         → TextraDbBridge.writeIncoming(...) etc.
           → (in YOUR app: writes a row into YOUR message store)

Everything below is just filling in those two chains.


================================================================
  EACH SMALI HOOK, AND ITS SOURCE-CODE REPLACEMENT
================================================================
TextRCS has exactly seven places where injected code meets the host
Textra app. Six are smali edits; one is a manifest entry. For each, here
is what TextRCS does and what you do instead.


--- HOOK 1 — OUTGOING SEND INTERCEPTION ---

WHAT TEXTRCS DOES
TextRCS patches Textra's last-mile SMS sender. In smali that is the
method `e5/d.smali::m(...)` — the line right before Textra would call
`SmsManager.sendMultipartTextMessage(...)`. The patch diverts to
`SendManager.sendSmsBridge(context, destination, parts, sentIntents)`,
which joins the multipart segments back into one body and calls
`SendManager.sendText(...)`. The real `SmsManager` call never runs, so
no cellular SMS goes out.

`SendManager.sendSmsBridge` exists ONLY as a smali entry point — it
adapts Textra's argument shapes. You do not need it.

WHAT YOU DO
You own your send code path, so there is nothing to "intercept" — you
just call the engine from it directly. In your app's send routine
(wherever the user's tapped "send" lands), instead of sending an SMS,
call:

    SendManager.get(context).sendText(recipientPhone, body, sentIntents)

`sentIntents` is optional. It exists because Textra reconciles a sent
message via a `PendingIntent` callback (the same contract
`SmsManager.sendMultipartTextMessage` uses). If your app marks a message
"sent" by some other means — a coroutine result, a callback, a DB
status flip — you can skip `sentIntents` entirely and instead make
`SendManager.sendText` return a result you act on. The shape of
`SendManager` is simple enough that adapting it to your callback style
is a few lines; the load-bearing call inside it is just
`RustBridge.sendText(appContext, recipientPhone, body)`.

NOTE on what `SendManager` does for you that is worth keeping:
  - It runs the send off the main thread (a single-thread executor).
  - It catches failures and reports success/failure back.
  - It has a "record-only" concept — see HOOK 6 below — so you do not
    re-transmit a message that another device already sent. Keep that.
You can also call `RustBridge.sendText(...)` straight from your own
code and drop `SendManager` if you prefer; `SendManager` is mostly a
threading + reconciliation wrapper.


--- HOOK 2 — RECEIVING + WRITING INCOMING MESSAGES ---

WHAT TEXTRCS DOES
There is no smali patch here — receiving is driven entirely by the
engine's callback. `RustBridge` implements `RustEventSink`; the engine
calls `onDataEvent(action, decryptedData, unencryptedData, isOld)` for
every long-poll frame. `RustBridge.Sink` parses `decryptedData` as the
`UpdateEvents` protobuf and hands it to
`IncomingMessageHandler.onUpdateEvents(context, events)`.

`IncomingMessageHandler` then, per message:
  - skips stale replays (`isOld`, and a `seenMessageIds` de-dup set —
    Google pushes the same message 2-3 times);
  - converts the timestamp (libgm sends microseconds; most stores
    want milliseconds);
  - resolves the real sender phone — a message carries only a libgm
    "participantID" (a short int like "3343"), NOT a phone number;
  - decides 1:1 vs group vs own-send vs status line;
  - calls the matching `TextraDbBridge.write*` method.

WHAT YOU DO
The `RustEventSink` implementation and `IncomingMessageHandler` are
NOT Textra-specific — they are honest protocol code. Reuse them almost
as-is. The ONLY thing you change is the final step: where
`IncomingMessageHandler` calls `TextraDbBridge.writeIncoming(...)`,
you call YOUR store's "insert an incoming message" function instead.

So your plan for receive:
  1. Keep `RustBridge` and its `Sink` class — it is your `RustEventSink`.
  2. Keep `IncomingMessageHandler` — the sender-resolution, de-dup,
     group detection, and own-send detection logic is all worth having
     and is hard to get right; do not rewrite it.
  3. Replace `TextraDbBridge` with your own adapter exposing the same
     handful of methods (see "THE STORE ADAPTER" section below).

Sender resolution detail worth understanding: `IncomingMessageHandler`
resolves a phone in priority order — the message's own
`senderParticipant`, then a `participantID → phone` cache it builds
from `ConversationEvent`s, then the conversation's primary participant,
and only as a last resort the raw participant int. If it cannot resolve
a real phone it HOLDS the message rather than threading it into a
junk conversation. Keep that behaviour; it prevents garbage threads.


--- HOOK 3 — GROUP CONVERSATIONS ---

WHAT TEXTRCS DOES
A group message has no 1:1 SMS form. TextRCS detects "group" when libgm
flags the conversation as a group OR it has more than one non-self
participant (`IncomingMessageHandler.cacheConversation`). For a group
TEXT it calls `TextraDbBridge.writeIncomingGroup(sender, allMembers,
text, ts)`; for group MEDIA, `writeIncomingGroupMms(...)`. Inside
`TextraDbBridge` these reach into Textra's own group-message writer —
heavy reflection — because that is the only way to make Textra thread a
message to a set of numbers when you cannot edit Textra.

WHAT YOU DO
The DECISION ("this is a group, here are the members") stays in
`IncomingMessageHandler` — keep it. The ACTION is just: write a message
into a conversation keyed by a SET of phone numbers instead of one. In a
source app that is trivial — your store already (or easily) supports a
multi-recipient conversation. So your store adapter exposes, say,
`writeIncomingGroup(senderPhone, memberPhones, body, ts)`, and it does
the obvious thing: find-or-create the conversation whose participant set
is `memberPhones`, insert a message from `senderPhone`. No reflection,
no PDUs.

(One product decision worth copying: key a group conversation purely by
its member set — one conversation per unique set of numbers. Trying to
give each libgm group its own separate thread leads to duplicate
conversations, because the conversation can only be re-found by its
member set.)


--- HOOK 4 — INLINE CONVERSATION-STATUS LINES (the "kind = 9" idea) ---

WHAT TEXTRCS DOES
Google Messages sends "status" / "tombstone" notices — "you created this
group", "now end-to-end encrypted", "Alex joined", etc. These must NOT
appear as chat bubbles. TextRCS detects them in
`IncomingMessageHandler.handleMessage` (a libgm `MessageStatusType`
value of 200 or higher) and calls `TextraDbBridge.writeSystemMessage`.

Because TextRCS cannot add a new view type to Textra's UI, it abuses an
EXISTING one: Textra's message-list adapter (`v6/A` in smali) already has
a centered "status line" row type — it uses the database column `kind`
with value `9`. So `writeSystemMessage` inserts a `messages` row with
`kind = 9`; Textra's adapter then renders it as a centered grey status
line for free.

WHAT YOU DO
The clean source-code version of "abuse an existing row type" is
"add a real row type". In your message-list adapter (`RecyclerView`
adapter or equivalent):
  - add a new view type, e.g. `VIEW_TYPE_STATUS`;
  - give it a simple centered-text layout;
  - return it from `getItemViewType(...)` for messages your store has
    flagged as status notices.
Then your store adapter exposes `writeSystemMessage(memberPhones, text,
ts)` which inserts a message row carrying that "this is a status notice"
flag. The `kind = 9` integer is a Textra implementation detail; in your
app it is just a boolean/enum on your own message model.

Detail to copy: TextRCS only inserts a status line if the conversation
ALREADY exists — it never creates a brand-new conversation just to hold
a tombstone. Do the same.


--- HOOK 5 — THE TYPING INDICATOR ---

WHAT TEXTRCS DOES
Two parts.

(a) Receiving the typing signal — no smali. `IncomingMessageHandler`
already parses `UpdateEvents`; when an event has a `typingEvent` it
calls `TypingIndicator.onTypingEvent(convID, userNumber, started)`.

(b) Knowing which conversation is on screen — ONE smali hook. TextRCS
adds a single `invoke-static` into Textra's `ConvoActivity` smali, in
the `onWindowFocusChanged(boolean)` method, calling
`TypingIndicator.onConvoFocus(this, hasFocus)`. That tells the indicator
which conversation screen is currently foregrounded so it shows the
"typing…" affordance on the right screen. (TextRCS uses
`onWindowFocusChanged` specifically because it is a non-final method
safe to extend; `onPause` is final in Textra and overriding it crashes.)

`TypingIndicator` also has to figure out the open conversation's phone
numbers, and in TextRCS it does that with guarded reflection into
Textra's private view model. It shows the affordance as a `Toast`
because injecting a view into Textra's live layout risks crashes.

WHAT YOU DO
(a) Receiving: keep `IncomingMessageHandler`'s typing branch — it is
fine as-is.

(b) On-screen tracking: no smali. In YOUR conversation Activity/Fragment
just call your typing logic from normal lifecycle methods —
`onResume()` / `onPause()`, or a `LifecycleObserver`. You already know
which conversation the screen is showing (you opened it), so you do not
need reflection to discover the phone numbers — pass them in directly.

And because it is your UI, you do not need the `Toast` workaround:
add a proper "typing…" row or header view to your chat screen. So in
your app `TypingIndicator` becomes much simpler — keep the event
plumbing (`onTypingEvent`, the auto-hide timeout, the
hide-on-message-arrived behaviour) and drop the reflection + Toast.


--- HOOK 6 — THE USER'S OWN SENDS FROM OTHER CLIENTS ---

WHAT TEXTRCS DOES
The user might send a message from their phone or from
messages.google.com. That message comes back down YOUR long-poll too.
It must be shown as an OUTGOING message in your app, not as an incoming
one — and it must NOT be re-transmitted (Google already sent it).

`IncomingMessageHandler.handleMessage` detects an own-send: it has no
`tmpID` (a tmpID is set only on messages this app itself sent) and
carries an outgoing status or an `isMe` sender. It routes those to
`handleOutgoing` → `TextraDbBridge.writeOutgoing(recipients, body, ts)`.

There is a subtle re-transmit trap. In Textra, `writeOutgoing` has to
go through Textra's own outgoing-send method to get a proper outgoing
row — but that method ALSO fires a real send. So before calling it,
`TextraDbBridge.writeOutgoing` calls
`SendManager.markRecordOnly(body)`; `SendManager.sendText` then sees the
"record-only" marker and skips the actual GM transmit (it just fires the
success callback). This is the "record-only" mechanism mentioned in
HOOK 1.

WHAT YOU DO
In a source app this is much simpler, because YOU control how a message
row is created — writing an outgoing row does not have to trigger a
send. So your store adapter's `writeOutgoing(recipients, body, ts)`
just inserts an outgoing message row and refreshes the UI. Done. No
re-transmit, so no "record-only" dance is needed at all.

KEEP, though, the DETECTION logic in `IncomingMessageHandler`
(no-tmpID + outgoing-status / isMe). That is real protocol knowledge and
is the same in any app. Also keep its handling of the race where an
own-send `MessageEvent` arrives before the `ConversationEvent` that
names the recipients — `IncomingMessageHandler` queues such a message
(`pendingOutgoing`) and flushes it once the participants are known.
That race is real and the queue is the fix.


--- HOOK 7 — PAIRING (Google sign-in) ---

WHAT TEXTRCS DOES
Pairing is a normal Android `Activity` — `PairingActivity` — so there is
no smali patch in the activity itself. The only host edit is how it is
LAUNCHED: TextRCS removed `PairingActivity`'s `LAUNCHER` intent-filter
(so the app opens to the conversation list, not the pairing screen) and
instead the Settings screen builds an explicit `Intent` to it (in
Textra's smali, "Settings → Pro User").

`PairingActivity` itself: shows a `WebView` pointed at Google's login
URL, watches the cookie jar for the `SAPISID` cookie to appear, then
hands off to the handshake code (`SignInGaiaClient` →
`GaiaPairingOrchestrator`), shows the confirmation emoji, and on success
saves the result through `SessionStore`. Once a session is saved it also
starts `ReceiveService`.

WHAT YOU DO
`PairingActivity` is ordinary Android code — drop it into your source
and adjust the package line. The launch is just your own navigation:
put a "Connect Google Messages" item in YOUR settings screen and
`startActivity(Intent(this, PairingActivity::class.java))`. Do NOT give
it a `LAUNCHER` filter — your app should open to its conversation list.

`PairingActivity` finds its layout and view IDs via
`resources.getIdentifier(...)` (a TextRCS trick to avoid depending on
the host's generated `R` class). In your own project you have a real
`R` class — you can switch those `resId(...)` lookups to ordinary
`R.layout.* / R.id.*` references, or leave them; both work.

The pairing flow produces a `GMessagesSession` which `SessionStore`
persists. That persisted session is exactly the input the engine needs
(Guide 1, step 5) — `RustBridge.start()` reads it back via
`SessionStore`, converts it to a `RustSession`, and connects the
`RustClient`. So pairing and the engine are already joined through
`SessionStore`; you do not wire them together yourself.

TELL YOUR USER HOW TO LOG IN — it is TWO taps, not one. When you hand
this integration over, output these steps to whoever will use the app,
and stress the second tap:
  1. Open the pairing screen and tap "Connect to Google Messages".
  2. Sign into the Google account in the WebView that opens.
  3. After the Google sign-in finishes, tap the Connect button a SECOND
     time. THIS tap starts the actual pairing handshake with the phone —
     the first tap only did the Google login. It is easy to miss.
  4. A confirmation emoji appears; on the phone running Google Messages,
     tap the matching emoji to finish pairing.


================================================================
  THE STORE ADAPTER — replacing TextraDbBridge
================================================================
`TextraDbBridge` is the one file you do NOT reuse. Every method in it is
reflection into Textra's private SQLite database (`messaging.db`) and
internal classes — synthesising fake SMS PDUs, inserting `mms_queue`
rows, calling obfuscated methods like `c5.d.U` and `r4.H.F0`. That is an
elaborate workaround for not having Textra's source. None of it applies
to you.

But `TextraDbBridge` is still a precise SPEC of what your own adapter
must do. Write a class — call it whatever your store suggests — exposing
these methods, each of which simply writes into YOUR database and
refreshes YOUR UI:

  writeIncoming(senderPhone, body, ts)
        Insert a 1:1 incoming text message.

  writeIncomingMms(context, senderPhone, text?, mediaBytes, mime, ts)
        Insert a 1:1 incoming message with an attachment.

  writeIncomingGroup(senderPhone, memberPhones, body, ts)
        Insert an incoming text into the group conversation keyed by
        `memberPhones`.

  writeIncomingGroupMms(context, senderPhone, toAddresses, text?,
                        mediaBytes?, mime?, ts)
        Group message with an attachment.

  writeOutgoing(recipientPhones, body, ts)
        Insert an OUTGOING message (the user's own send from another
        device — see HOOK 6).

  writeSystemMessage(context, memberPhones, text, ts)
        Insert a status-notice row (see HOOK 4) — only if that
        conversation already exists.

Keep the SIGNATURES the same, so `IncomingMessageHandler` (which you ARE
reusing) calls your adapter unchanged — just point its
`TextraDbBridge.*` calls at your class. The BODIES are all yours and, in
a real app with its own store, each is short: find-or-create a
conversation, insert a row, notify your UI.

Media note: incoming MMS attachment bytes are produced by
`RustBridge.downloadMedia(context, mediaId, key)` — the engine downloads
and AES-GCM-decrypts the file and hands you the raw bytes (e.g. a JPEG).
`IncomingMessageHandler` already calls this off-thread for you; your
`writeIncomingMms` just receives the finished `ByteArray` and saves it
wherever your app keeps attachments.


================================================================
  THE STEPS, IN ORDER
================================================================
Do these AFTER Guide 1 (the engine builds and `RustClient` imports).

1. COPY THE GLUE CLASSES INTO YOUR SOURCE TREE.
   The full app-side source is in this kit under
   `App Integration Source/com/textrcs/`.
   From it, copy: `bridge/RustBridge.kt`,
   `send/SendManager.kt`, `receive/ReceiveService.kt`,
   `receive/IncomingMessageHandler.kt`, `ui/TypingIndicator.kt`,
   `ui/pairing/PairingActivity.kt`, the whole `protocol/` package and
   the whole `gmproto/` package, and `control/Hooks.kt` (it is inert
   but the others import it). Adjust `package` lines to your project.
   You will delete/replace some of these later — copy them first so it
   compiles, then refine.

2. WRITE YOUR STORE ADAPTER.
   Create your replacement for `TextraDbBridge` with the six method
   signatures listed above, each writing into YOUR message store. Do
   NOT copy `TextraDbBridge.kt` itself — it is all Textra reflection.

3. REPOINT IncomingMessageHandler AT YOUR ADAPTER.
   In `IncomingMessageHandler.kt` (and in `ReceiveService`'s self-test,
   if you keep it), change every `TextraDbBridge.write*` call to your
   adapter's method. Nothing else in that file needs to change — the
   sender resolution, de-dup, group and own-send logic all stay.

4. REGISTER ReceiveService IN YOUR MANIFEST.
   Add `ReceiveService` as a `<service>` with a foreground-service type
   appropriate to your app. It keeps the process (and the long-poll)
   alive while backgrounded. Start it once a session exists — the
   natural place is right after pairing succeeds (PairingActivity
   already does this) and at app start if already paired (see step 5).
   CAVEAT — Google Messages must be the phone's default SMS app: this
   app is a companion to Google Messages on the user's phone. For the
   relay to stay fed in the background and deliver notifications
   reliably, Google Messages must be set as the default SMS app on that
   phone — if it is not, RCS is not fully active there. The reference
   build does not handle this. Add first-run onboarding that tells the
   user to set Google Messages as their phone's default SMS app and
   walks them through it.

5. START THE ENGINE AT APP LAUNCH IF ALREADY PAIRED.
   In your `Application.onCreate()` (or a `ContentProvider.onCreate`,
   which runs even earlier — that is what TextRCS's
   `CrashCatcherProvider` does), check `SessionStore(app).load()`; if a
   session exists, start `ReceiveService`. Without this, an
   already-paired user who opens the app to the conversation list would
   have a closed long-poll and receive nothing.

6. ADD A "CONNECT GOOGLE MESSAGES" ENTRY POINT.
   In your settings screen, add an item that does
   `startActivity(Intent(this, PairingActivity::class.java))`. Do not
   give `PairingActivity` a `LAUNCHER` intent-filter.

7. WIRE YOUR SEND BUTTON.
   In your app's send code path, call
   `SendManager.get(context).sendText(recipientPhone, body, sentIntents?)`
   (or `RustBridge.sendText(...)` directly). Drop the `sentIntents`
   argument if your app reconciles "sent" some other way; adapt
   `SendManager` to return a result instead.

8. WIRE THE TYPING INDICATOR (optional, do last).
   Simplify `TypingIndicator`: keep `onTypingEvent` and the auto-hide
   logic; drop the reflection and the `Toast`. From your conversation
   screen's `onResume`/`onPause`, tell it which conversation is open
   (pass the phone numbers directly — no reflection needed). Render the
   affordance as a real view in your chat screen.

9. ADD A STATUS-LINE ROW TYPE (optional).
   In your message-list adapter, add a centered "status notice" view
   type and have your store adapter's `writeSystemMessage` insert rows
   your adapter renders with it. This is the clean version of TextRCS's
   `kind = 9` trick.

10. BUILD AND TEST AFTER EACH STEP.
    Pairing first (step 6) — confirm a session is saved. Then receive
    (steps 3-5) — confirm an incoming message lands in your store. Then
    send (step 7). Typing and status lines last.


================================================================
  KEEPING THE RELAY ALIVE IN THE BACKGROUND
================================================================
The relay only delivers messages while its long-poll connection is
open. Android actively works against a background process holding a
live connection, so this needs deliberate handling — it is the single
hardest part of shipping a relay like this reliably, and it is easy to
under-estimate.

WHAT THE REFERENCE BUILD DOES (reuse all of this)
- `ReceiveService` is a FOREGROUND service. A plain background service
  or a bare worker thread is killed within minutes once the app leaves
  the screen, and the long-poll dies with it — messages simply stop
  arriving. The foreground service, with its ongoing notification, is
  what keeps the process and the connection alive. It is not optional.
- It returns `START_STICKY` from `onStartCommand`, so if Android kills
  the process under memory pressure the service is recreated — at which
  point it re-opens the connection.
- It is started early and from more than one place: immediately after
  pairing succeeds, AND at app launch if a session already exists. The
  reference build does the launch-time start from a `ContentProvider`,
  which runs before `Application.onCreate`. Without that launch start,
  an already-paired user who just opens the app to the conversation
  list has no open long-poll and receives nothing until they send.

WHAT YOU STILL HAVE TO HANDLE (Android / OEM realities)
- Doze and App Standby: when the device is idle the OS suspends network
  and defers background work. A foreground service is the supported way
  to hold a live connection through this — there is no way around
  needing one.
- Foreground-service types: Android 14+ requires the service to declare
  a `foregroundServiceType` and hold the matching permission. Choose a
  type honest to what the service does.
- OEM battery managers: Samsung, Xiaomi / MIUI, Huawei, OnePlus and
  others ship aggressive task-killers that go beyond the standard rules
  and will kill even a foreground service. No API defeats this. The
  only mitigation is onboarding: ask the user to exempt the app from
  battery optimization (the system "ignore battery optimizations"
  request) and, on the worst OEMs, to allow it in the vendor's own
  battery / auto-start settings. Be honest with the user that without
  this, background delivery can be delayed or dropped.
- Non-default-SMS limits: a non-default-SMS app is given less
  background latitude — see step 4's caveat about Google Messages.
- Process death is normal — assume the process WILL be killed
  sometimes. `START_STICKY` plus re-opening the engine on recreate is
  the recovery path; there is no way to never be killed.

Treat reliable background delivery as its own milestone, not a free
side effect of starting a foreground service.


HOW TO TELL IT'S WORKING
------------------------
  - Pairing: after the WebView Google login and the emoji confirmation,
    `SessionStore` has a saved session and `ReceiveService`'s ongoing
    notification appears.
  - Receive: send yourself a text from another phone — it should appear
    in the right conversation in your store within a second or two, with
    the correct sender and timestamp (not a microsecond-scale timestamp,
    and not threaded under a numeric "participantID").
  - Send: a message sent from your app reaches the recipient and your
    UI flips it to "sent".
  - Own-sends: a message you send from your phone shows up in your app
    as an OUTGOING message — and the recipient does NOT receive a
    duplicate.
